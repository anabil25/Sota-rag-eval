758-1          CHILD SUPPORT INCOME

The requirement to sign over any child support payments to the state and to cooperate with the Child Support Enforcement Division (CSED) is described in [ATAP](#)Alaska Temporary Assistance Program [MS 717](../717/717_child_support_assignment_and_cooperation.md). Families may receive up to $50 a month as pass-through payments from the child support collected (see [MS 717-7 A](../717/717-7_how_csed_collections_affect_the_temporary_assistance_case.md#717-7_A._CHILD_SUPPORT_PASS_THROUGH_PROVISIONS)). In some cases, [CSED](#)Child Support Enforcement Division may issue child support payments directly to the family for months the family was not on [TA](#)Temporary Assistance.

Child support payments received by applicants and recipients are processed as follows:

Payments received before application approval: Count any child support payment received and kept by an applicant, less the pass-through amount of up to $50, to determine eligibility and the amount of the assistance payment (use the "CH" code in [EIS](#)Eligibility Information System). The child support may be received from a child support enforcement agency or directly from a noncustodial parent.

Payments received after approval: After [TA](#)Temporary Assistance is approved, [CSED](#)Child Support Enforcement Division should receive any child support paid. If it is expected that child support payments will continue, the expected amount, less the pass-through amount of up to $50, is counted in determining prospective eligibility (use the "CS" code in [EIS](#)Eligibility Information System). The child support amount is disregarded in the assistance payment calculation, as described in [ATAP](#)Alaska Temporary Assistance Program [MS 717-7 B](../717/717-7_how_csed_collections_affect_the_temporary_assistance_case.md#717-7_B._PROSPECTIVE_ELIGIBILITY_DECISION).

758-1 A.      ASSIGNED CHILD SUPPORT

- A financial penalty may be imposed and a protective payee selected if a family keeps child support payments from the noncustodial parent after [TA](#)Temporary Assistance is approved.
- If the payments could have been anticipated, the amount of the payment, less the $50 pass-through amount, must be counted to determine if an incorrect assistance payment has been issued.
- Child support payments kept by the family, less the $50 pass-through amount, must be counted as income in the payment calculation for future months.
- Child support payments count as income to the child for whom the payments are intended.
- Child support contributions that are made in-kind rather than as cash are not counted as income.

758-1 B.      NON-ASSIGNED CHILD SUPPORT

- The family may keep all [CSED](#)Child Support Enforcement Division payments for child support collected for months the family was not receiving [TA](#)Temporary Assistance. [CSED](#)Child Support Enforcement Division payments must be reported by the family to [DPA](#)Division of Public Assistance as unearned income.
- Child support payments kept by the family, less the $50 pass-through amount, must be counted as income in the payment calculation for future months.

Example:

Malcolm Reynolds is a single parent with 3 children. He receives a $50 pass-through child support payment from [DPA](#)Division of Public Assistance each month. On January 8 he reports he received $115 from [CSED](#)Child Support Enforcement Division. On February 9 he reports that he received $105 from [CSED](#)Child Support Enforcement Division. On March 5 he reports he received $120 from [CSED](#)Child Support Enforcement Division. The payments can be anticipated. Since Mr. Reynolds is already receiving $50 pass-through payments each month for the collections [CSED](#)Child Support Enforcement Division kept, the caseworker prospects $115 ($115 plus $110 plus $120 divided by 3 months) of unearned child support income for the family.
